HIV [DNA] Detecting POC Devices

Main Highlights:

  • Sample Preparation
    • DNA Sample Preparation From Blood [Review Points out that the process involves a lot of technical Difficulties]
      • Plasma Separation
        • Plasma separation is not required for molecular diagnostics but is important for HIV viral load - inclusion of lymphocytes overestimates the viral load.
        • Clinical laboratories separate serum and plasma from blood via centrifugation, which might not be applicable for POC devices.
        • Separation methods include: capillary imbibition, on-chip centrifugation, blood cell sedimentation, cross-flow filtration, etc. Note: either produce too small plasma volume or require high blood dilution
      • Dry Sample Preservation [For cases which include collection of samples and then testing in lab]
        • Blood samples are sometimes preserved on cellulose-based filters - dried blood spots DBS, which has limitations
        • SlipChip-based microfluidic device uses stabilization matrices and successfully achieves the preservation of the sample

      • Nucleic Acid Extraction
        • In general, conventional nucleic acids are extracted from complex samples, using centrifugation, ion exchange, liquid-liquid extraction (i.e., phenol chloroform), ethanol precipitation, and silica solid-phase extraction (i.e., spin- column). This methods are not that much applicable for POC devices.
        • Filter Membrane-Based Extraction [Elaborated in Paper]
        • Magnetic Beads based Extraction [Elaborated in Paper]
  • Isothermal Nucleic Acid Amplification Methods: LAMP, RPA, HDA, NASBA, RT-LAMP
    • LAMP is the most widely used requires around 60-65 degrees of Celsius.
    • RPA does not require precise temperature control, allows instrumentation free amplification, has high tolerance towards impure samples.
  • Portable Incubators
    • Portable electronic processor (Fig. 3A) for HIV viral load testing by using their nuclemeter chip platform. The processor consists of a flexible, polyimide-based, thin film heater, an electronic circuit board, and batteries. The processor is powered by four AAA batteries and is capable of maintaining the chip at a constant temperature (i.e. ~63C) for RT-LAMP amplification and can work continuously for more than 10 hours without replacing the batteries
    • The temperature of the chemical heater was regulated by a phase change in the material.     • Simple sodium acetate trihydrate (SAT) heater (Fig. 3C) to incubate RPA assays for infant HIV diagnostics. These SAT heaters are rechargeable by heating at 80 째C for 20 minutes. The heat for resetting the SAT heaters can be provided by exothermic chemical reaction, such as water-activated flameless ration heater (Mg-Fe alloy/ water)
  • Detection Technologies
    • Portable fluorescent reader to monitor the fluorescence emission of their isothermal amplification reactor for HIV viral load testing in real time. The reader consists of a light-emitting diode (LED) as the excitation light source and a photodiode for fluorescence detection.
    • A portable USB microscope (Fig. 3A) [90] was used to monitor in real time the fluorescence emission of multiple isothermal amplification reactors.
    • Damhorst et al. [94] used high-output blue LED to excite the fluorescent dye of their microfluidic chip and a smartphone camera to record fluorescence emission of the amplification reaction in real time. A smartphone flashlight could function as the excitation source to eliminate the need for a separate light source [95].
    • LFA coupled with amplification methods to detect the amplicons.
